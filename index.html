Social Order

Originally, Benin City was an aggregate of small settlements which had allegiance to the king.
Around 1000s, society was hierarchical. It was headed by a succession of kings, called the Ogisos (sky rulers), And the king was assisted by the Uzamas (powerful nobles).
Around 1300, the Edo people felt that the ogiso was no longer an effective leader. Oranmiyan created a new dynasty, calling himself the first Oba of Benin, and so the Obas' regime began. The Obas would rule Benin for the next six centuries.

Social Organisation

The basic organising principle within both the village and the urban ward is the division of the population into age sets. Pre Colonial Benin society had a clearly demarcated class structure: a mostly urban elite, comprising the governmental, religious, and educational bureaucracies; a commoner group, consisting of lower-status urbanites, such as artisans; and the peasantry. Formerly, the king and chiefs had slaves, primarily acquired through warfare, who constituted an agricultural workforce for the elite.

The socio-political organisation exists at two levels; the central and the provincial.
The managerial system in pre-colonial Benin revolved around the oba and his royal court. From the palace, all directives were issued for onward transmission to all parts of the kingdom.
